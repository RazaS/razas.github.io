counterfactuals

The happiest people I know are not the smartest or the sharpest decision makers. They are the best at resisting fantasy and savoring reality.

This is hard to do because choice deprives us of the unchosen. Picking one-out-of-four leaves three restaurants untried, three books unread, and three lovers unloved, so that by construction our unchosen lives usually outnumber the chosen. The roads not taken are “counterfactuals”— things that could have happened, but did not, and so leave a residue of regret. This regret is out of proportion because there are more things to regret than enjoy. We are also adept at sensing what is bad about lived reality, and relatively inept at imagining what would have been bad about unlived counterfactuals. This bias runs deep and deterministically leads to regret. Therein lies the allure of greener grasses.

Being at peace with our choices then becomes a matter of negotiating this asymmetry. The happiest people I know are incredibly good at managing counterfactuals. They are deliberate about recognizing their actual fortunes and careful when considering unrealized fortunes. They also walk a careful line between the naive pangloss and the tired nihilist, avoiding either excess. Of course, there is more to happiness than how we think about choices, and the pursuit of happiness is itself a decision not a presupposition. Whatever you choose, choose wholeheartedly.
