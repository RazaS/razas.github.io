On this bright sunny Sunday, I want to wax philosophical on long working hours. There’s a classic study done with lumberjacks who were offered either to chop wood with sharp axes for their current wage, or blunt axes for a much higher wage, and they all chose sharp axes because it’s boring to do it the other way. This is a specific case of something more universal: intrinsic motivators are stronger than extrinsic motivators. Many companies know this and often use it to exploit people who work very hard not for salary or notoriety, but for the promise of self-fulfillment.

The motivational effect of finding meaning in our work is so strong it creates paradoxes. Increasing an employee salary can sometimes lead to “motivational crowding” and cause people to work less hard than if they were motivated by fulfillment alone. The theory is that giving people an external reason to work (e.g. more money) disincentivizes the construction of internal reasons to find their task meaningful.

The business of harnessing intrinsic motivation can become rather insidious. You know this first hand if you have overworked at non-profits or similar enterprises that boast a “higher calling”. They are often sustained by extraordinary individual sacrifice, and have either short lifespans or high turnover of scapegoats to the greater good. This is pernicious for a few reasons. Burnout, when regarded a necessary occupational hazard of moral work, becomes an alibi to exploit people. This is seen in many workers at Tesla Motors. for instance, who in a spectacular effort to save humanity find themselves dreadfully overtaxed and distanced from their own. Inevitably, burned out people do a bad job and systems collapse. Designers take pride in their work being “so good it’s invisible”, because good systems have banal excellence and shouldn’t rely on extraordinary individual effort. Overclocking intrinsic motivation will eventually undermine its own purpose. Moral work, if we truly deem it important, should be made easier until success becomes ordinary.
