loss

When you lose something close to you, the loss is rarely singular, rarely follows neat stages of grief. Loss and its grief look more like a series of related losses that unravel gradually and cause fresh grief each time. When you lose someone, you lose them once, and then again each time they are missing somewhere they should have been. When you lose a part of yourself, it follows many losses of what could have been done and who you could have become. This is the more common nature of loss which feels less like a catastrophic thump, more like waves of sorrow each unpredictable in what it will sweep away. Enduring each wave at a time helps because to confront the ocean in entirety may wipe you out. Changing course helps because while the new path may be tumultuous, it frees you of the wistful and aching familiarity of the original. You can rarely end a loss, though it can end you, and more often you will weather it or turn the other way. Each loss also clears space for some new choice that may have once been unnecessary or unthinkable before. And each wave of sorrow following loss becomes a way to understand your choice until it can become familiar, slowly indistinguishable from the rest.
